The Last Interview

November 10, 2025 • By Arham Qureshi

journalism regret memory fame humanity Interviews

The house stood alone on the hill — half-covered in vines, half-swallowed by silence. Inside, Sahir Abbas, once Pakistan’s most celebrated director, sat waiting with a cup of cold tea.

When Aisha Karim arrived, recorder in hand, she felt a nervous respect. She’d grown up watching his films — the kind that made you ache quietly after the credits. He hadn’t given an interview in fifteen years.

“Let’s talk about your last movie,” she began softly. “The one you never finished.”

Sahir smiled faintly. “Ah, The River Without End. You’ve done your homework.” He leaned back, eyes drifting to the window. “It was supposed to be a love story. Two people meeting on opposite sides of a river every spring — until one year, the river doesn’t thaw.”

She clicked her pen. “Why didn’t you finish it?”

He hesitated. “Because she didn’t return.”

Aisha frowned. “The actress?”

He nodded slowly. “My wife. She was my muse — my reason to tell stories. The day she disappeared, the river scene was being filmed. I told myself I’d wait until she came back to finish it.” He looked at the recorder. “And I kept waiting.”

Silence stretched between them, deep and fragile. Aisha whispered, “Maybe the movie wasn’t meant to end — maybe it is your story.”

For the first time, Sahir smiled — a real, unguarded one. “Perhaps,” he said. “And maybe today, it finally ends.”

He reached across the table and stopped the recorder himself.

The next morning, when Aisha returned to deliver the interview notes, the caretaker told her: “He passed away in his sleep. Peaceful. Smiling.”

When she played the recording again that night, she noticed something new — a faint sound at the end. A woman’s laughter, blending softly with the rustle of the wind.

Meaning / Reflection:

Sometimes, the story we’re trying to capture already ended — and all we can do is listen to what remains between the words.
