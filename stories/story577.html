Echoes Off Air

November 18, 2025 • By Nida Haroon

music loss identity memory truth

The rain came down in quiet sheets as Hassan Malik set up his recorder on the scratched wooden desk of Station 91.3. The air smelled of dust, metal, and faint electricity — the scent of forgotten stories.

Across from him sat Farid Rahman, once the nation’s most beloved radio host — his voice had been the comfort of millions, his laughter the pulse of a generation. But tonight, he looked smaller, as though time itself had been whispering secrets into his bones.

“Mr. Farid, thank you for agreeing to this,” Hassan said softly, pressing record.

The old man smiled. “People stopped calling me ‘Mr.’ a long time ago. Just Farid is fine.”

For the first few minutes, they spoke about radio’s golden age — how it could paint worlds with nothing but sound. But when Hassan mentioned the last broadcast — the one that had cut off mid-sentence twenty years ago — the warmth left Farid’s eyes.

“It wasn’t a technical failure,” Farid said quietly. “It was me. I stopped speaking.”

Hassan leaned in. “Why?”

“Because I realized I’d been lying,” Farid whispered. “Every night I told people to hold on, that everything would be alright. But that night, my daughter called the station — crying. I told her I was too busy to talk. The line went dead. So did she, an hour later.”

His voice cracked like static. “After that, every word felt dishonest.”

For a moment, all Hassan could hear was the rain against the glass — steady, relentless, forgiving.

“Then why speak now?” he asked gently.

Farid looked at the red ON AIR sign flickering above them. “Because silence is also a lie,” he said. “And I’ve been quiet long enough.”

He reached across the console and pushed the old broadcast button. A faint hum filled the room. Then, with a trembling breath, Farid said into the microphone:

“If anyone is listening — forgive the voices that went missing. Some of us needed to lose words to learn what they meant.”

Hassan sat frozen as the red light steadied — glowing brighter than it had in decades.

The next morning, the radio station played that recording on every frequency — one last time. And for the first time in years, people stopped scrolling, stopped rushing, and simply listened.

Meaning / Reflection:
Words hold power, but silence carries truth. Farid reminds us that every story we tell — or avoid — shapes the sound of who we are. Some confessions don’t echo until the air itself is ready to listen.
