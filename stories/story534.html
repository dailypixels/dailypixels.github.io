Shadows of the Forgotten Well

July 9, 2025 • Amina Rauf

Mystery Folklore Heritage

In the heart of Chakwal, there stood an ancient well, long sealed and surrounded by silence. Elders spoke of it in hushed tones — a place where secrets sank, where whispers of betrayal echoed in water too deep for light to touch. Children dared not approach it, for the stories had carved fear into their imagination.

When Amina, a young journalist, returned to her ancestral village, she noticed the well cordoned off with weathered planks. Curiosity tugged at her. Why had this once communal source of life been abandoned? She interviewed villagers, collecting fragments of memory. Some said a child had drowned, others claimed a feud between families cursed the water, and a few insisted the well swallowed confessions no one dared to hear aloud.

That night, unable to sleep, Amina visited the courtyard. The summer air was heavy, and the cicadas’ chorus masked her footsteps. She pressed her ear to the boards. A faint sound rose — not water, but a low echo like voices layered upon each other. She stumbled back, unsettled. Was it wind? Or memory?

The next morning, she dug into local archives. A record from 1935 revealed the truth: the well had been the site of executions during colonial unrest. Rebels were silenced, their bodies hidden beneath the water. The well was not cursed; it was burdened with unmarked history.

Amina published her story with care, framing the well not as a place of fear but as a graveyard that demanded remembrance. The villagers, at first reluctant, gathered for a prayer circle. Lanterns lit the courtyard, and for the first time in decades, silence was broken with song. The well remained sealed, but its story was no longer buried.

As Amina left the village, she realized the shadows around the well had not been supernatural at all — they were the shadows of forgetting. And by naming them, she had brought them into light.
