The Last Train Home

July 8, 2025 • Alex Rivera

Drama Mystery Twist

The station was nearly silent, save for the hum of a vending machine and the faint squeak of rats somewhere in the shadows. I clutched my ticket tightly—“Platform 3, Last Train, 11:58 PM.” There were no announcements, no staff, no other passengers. Just me, and the dim glow of the clock ticking toward midnight.

The train screeched into view right on time, its headlights piercing through the fog. The doors slid open with a hiss, revealing an empty carriage. I hesitated for a moment, then stepped inside. The smell of steel and rain filled the air as I sank into a worn leather seat near the window.

The train jolted forward, gliding through a city swallowed by darkness. Streetlights flickered outside, but I couldn’t make out the shapes of buildings—just shadows, blurring together as if the city had been erased. I checked my phone: no signal. The battery icon flashed red.

At the next stop, an old man in a tattered coat stepped in. He sat across from me, clutching a briefcase that looked older than him. He smiled faintly, his eyes tired. “You’re taking the last train,” he said softly. “Not many do.” I nodded awkwardly, unsure what to say.

Stop after stop, more passengers boarded—silent figures, their faces pale and distant. A woman in a wedding dress. A boy clutching a broken toy. A man in a firefighter’s uniform, his helmet resting in his lap. None of them spoke. They simply stared out the windows into the black void rushing past.

“Strange night,” I whispered to the old man. He turned to me slowly, his voice gentle. “It always is. The first night is the hardest.” I frowned. “First night of what?” He didn’t answer.

The train began to slow. The conductor’s voice finally crackled over the intercom: “Next stop, Last Stop.” His tone was calm, almost reverent. The old man stood, motioning for me to follow. “It’s time.”

I stepped off the train onto a platform bathed in golden light. The air was warm, and I could hear faint music in the distance, like a lullaby. The old man gestured to a long staircase leading upward. “You’ll be alright,” he said softly. “We all get here eventually.”

Confused, I turned back to the train, but it was already gone. The platform was empty. Slowly, it dawned on me. I didn’t remember buying the ticket. I didn’t remember leaving my apartment. I didn’t even remember how I got to the station.

My heart pounded as I looked toward the glowing staircase. And then I remembered the crash—the sound of screeching tires, the blinding headlights, the silence that followed. I took a shaky breath, wiped my tears, and started climbing.
